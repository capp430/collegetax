Tax Glossary & Filing Guide

This page explains the key terms used in the calculator and walks you through how federal and state tax filing works for college students.

W-2 Form

A W-2 is issued by your employer at the end of the year. It reports total wages earned (Box 1) and how much federal income tax was withheld (Box 2). It may also show Social Security and Medicare taxes paid. You need this form to file your federal return.

1099 Form

If you worked freelance or contract jobs, you may receive a 1099 form instead of a W-2. Taxes are usually NOT withheld from 1099 income, which means you may owe taxes at filing time.

Filing Status

Your tax category (Single, Head of Household, etc.). Filing status affects your standard deduction and tax brackets. Most college students file as Single unless they qualify as Head of Household.

Standard Deduction (2025)

A fixed IRS amount that reduces taxable income. For 2025, the standard deduction is higher than prior years due to inflation adjustments. Most students use the standard deduction instead of itemizing.

Taxable Income

Your income after subtracting the standard deduction. Federal income tax is calculated on this remaining amount.

Marginal Tax Rate

The tax rate applied to your last dollar earned. The U.S. tax system is progressive, meaning income is taxed in layers or “brackets.”

Withholding

Money your employer already sent to the IRS throughout the year. If too much was withheld, you receive a refund. If too little was withheld, you may owe money.

Self-Employment Tax

If you earn freelance income, you may owe self-employment tax (15.3%) to cover Social Security and Medicare contributions.

Dependent

If someone claims you as a dependent (often a parent), it may affect certain credits and eligibility. However, you may still need to file your own return.

Refund vs Amount Owed

A refund means you paid more in withholding than your actual tax liability. Owing means your withholding did not cover your tax responsibility.

State Income Taxes

In addition to federal taxes, many states require their own income tax filing. State tax rules vary widely.

States with NO state income tax:
Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, Wyoming, South Dakota, Alaska.

All other states generally have some form of income tax. This matters because:

  • You may need to file a separate state return.
  • State withholding appears on your W-2.
  • Refunds or amounts owed may differ between federal and state filings.
  • Some states have flat tax rates, others use progressive brackets.

This calculator estimates federal income tax only. State filing requirements must be checked separately.

Step-by-Step Filing Overview

  1. Gather Documents: W-2 forms, any 1099 forms, Social Security number, and bank information for direct deposit.
  2. Choose a Filing Platform:
    • IRS Free File (if eligible)
    • FreeTaxUSA
    • TurboTax
    • H&R Block
  3. Enter Income Information: Input your W-2 and 1099 details exactly as shown.
  4. Review Deductions & Credits: Most students use the standard deduction.
  5. Review Refund or Amount Owed: Confirm all numbers before submitting.
  6. Submit Electronically: E-filing is faster and typically processes within 21 days.

Federal tax returns are generally due April 15.

If you do not file when required, penalties and interest may apply.
