Tax Glossary & Filing Guide

W-2 Form

A W-2 is issued by your employer and reports your total wages earned and federal/state taxes withheld during the year. You use this form to enter income and withholding when filing your tax return.

Standard Deduction

A fixed IRS amount that reduces taxable income before tax is calculated. Most college students use the standard deduction rather than itemizing.

Taxable Income

The amount remaining after deductions that is actually taxed using federal tax brackets.

Withholding

Taxes taken out of your paycheck during the year. If too much is withheld, you receive a refund. If too little is withheld, you may owe money.

Self-Employment Income

Income earned from freelance or contract work (often reported on a 1099). This income is subject to both income tax and self-employment tax.

Marginal Tax Rate

The rate applied to your last dollar earned. The U.S. uses progressive brackets, meaning higher income portions are taxed at higher rates.

State Income Taxes

In addition to federal taxes, many states require their own income tax filing. State tax rules vary widely and may use different brackets or flat rates.

States with NO state income tax:
Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, Wyoming, South Dakota, Alaska.

Why this matters:

  • You may need to file a separate state return.
  • State withholding appears on your W-2.
  • Refunds or amounts owed may differ between federal and state filings.
  • Some states use flat taxes, others use progressive brackets.

This calculator estimates federal income tax only. State filing requirements must be checked separately.

Step-by-Step Filing Overview

  1. Gather Documents: W-2 forms, any 1099 forms, Social Security number, and bank information for direct deposit.
  2. Choose a Filing Platform: IRS Free File (if eligible), FreeTaxUSA, TurboTax, or H&R Block.
  3. Enter Income Information: Input W-2 and 1099 details exactly as shown.
  4. Review Deductions & Credits: Most students use the standard deduction.
  5. Review Refund or Amount Owed: Confirm numbers before submitting.
  6. Submit Electronically: E-filing is faster and typically processes within 21 days.
Federal tax returns are generally due April 15. If you do not file when required, penalties and interest may apply.
